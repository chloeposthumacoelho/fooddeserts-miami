What is Palm Oil?

    Palm oil, a derivative of the African oil palm plant, is the “world’s highest yielding and least expensive vegetable oil” (Vijay et al, 2016) which has led the oil to becoming a highly universal and popularly used commodity throughout the world. Towards the end of 2018 and into 2019, palm oil evolved into being an incredibly popular point of debate – with social media campaigns such as Iceland’s “#NoPalmOilChristmas’ advert going viral on social media and on mainstream media. Despite palm oil recently gaining significant momentum and exposure throughout popular culture, the issue of palm oil is not a new issue. Below is a video from the BBC which provides an introductory description into palm oil:



Products have been adapted from the African oil plant for “thousands of years”, however, “in the last 50 years … there has been a phenomenal expansion in its cultivation throughout the tropics, such that palm oil is now a major commodity of world trade, and oil palm is a leading source of vegetable oil” (Henson et al., 2012: 2). The rapid gain in popularity of the oil is fundamentally due to the benefit of palm oil being cheap, efficient, and a highly versatile oil – influencing the oil to be found and used in thousands of products.

Why is Palm Oil an Issue?

    The use of palm oil is a significant moral issue due to the damage the production of the product has on the environment, local communities and rare animals. Such damage associated with the growth of palm has led the oil to be named “cruel oil or green gold” as “the oil palm industry (planting and milling) has a devastating impact on the environment and ecosystems” (Khatun et al., 2017: 608). In short, the production of palm oil requires large amounts of land to be deforested – leading to habitat loss, conflict within indigenous communities, death of rare animals, and carbon pollution due to ‘slash and burn’ deforestation techniques (Khatun et al., 2017: 608). The various ethical consequences associated with the production of palm oil are discussed in more detail throughout this essay, including - carbon pollution, mass deforestation, death of rare animals and loss of habitat for indigenous communities.
