Introduction


   Arguably, the most discussed angle of the palm oil debate is the impact of palm oil production on rare animals (claim), evidenced in the growing popularity of campaigns, such as Iceland's viral Christmas advert(reason). The widespread awareness that has been raised as a result of consumers learning about the what palm oil is and the impact it has on the environment has been highly beneficial to the palm oil debate - evidenced with the discussion being brought to mainstream media and social media: both highly accessible platforms for consumers to be educated. While the media attention that has been brought to the palm oil debate has been highly beneficial in starting the debate and engaging consumers with the emotional side of palm oil production, there are very few sources which fully discuss the impact of palm oil production on animals in an online multimedia format. This section of this multimedia essay shall outline what animals are affected and how they are affected - highlighting the importance of consumers taking steps to reduce their support of palm oil.



What Animals are Affected?


   A consistent theme with all areas of the palm oil debate is that it is not only the issue of wildlife being destroyed, but the wildlife that is being destroyed is highly valuable. This theme also extends to the impact palm oil production has on animals as it is mainly rare animals which suffer the most. As highlighted by The Orangutan Project, the animals which are most affected in Indonesia include "the world's most unique and iconic endangered wildlife such as the orangutan, elephant and tiger" (The Orangutan Project, 2016). To experience the severity of the effect palm oil is having on rare animals, please watch this video:


Video from The Dodo discussing the rare animals that are affected by palm oil production and deforestation. (Source).


Animals are impacted by palm oil production due to the deforestation required to grow and maintain the crop. For orangutans, death is likely due to production companies eradicating large amounts of trees - where orangutans live. Animals such as elephants and tigers are impacted due to the harsh deforestation processes often using fire (as discussed in 'Carbon Pollution') and for animals that survive, life is much more difficult due to their homes and natural food sources being destroyed and replaced with rows of palm crops.

Where are Rare Animals most Affected?


Orangutans


   Borneo in particular is a Southeast Asian island in which orangutans have significantly suffered as a result of palm oil production, largely due to Indonesia being the world’s biggest exporter of palm oil. Presented by (Voigt et al., 2018), “50% of Bornean orang-utans were affected by natural resource extraction” and also that “100,000 Bornean orangutans were lost between 1999 and 2015” (Voigt et al., 2018). The study also importantly points out that many orangutangs are killed due to hunting which is a critical issue to be considered alongside palm oil deforestation.


For a full-length documentary highlighting the severity of the suffering that is experienced by orangutans throughout areas such as Indonesia and Malaysia due to palm oil, please find the BBC Panorama documentary below:


"In Panorama’s Dying For a Biscuit, reporter Raphael Rowe journeys into the rainforest of Borneo, where he uncovers evidence of palm oil companies cutting down trees illegally and developing plantations on protected land" (Source).




Elephants


    Throughout Southeast Asian countries where palm oil production rates are the highest in the world, rare breeds of elephants are suffering and at risk of extinction due to the processes involved with the growth of palm oil. Aside from the effects of general deforestation and loss of habitat, an unfortunate practice of palm oil workers poisoning elephants has been reported. The practice is said to take place because "that's how the plantation workers stop the animals from eating the fruit of the oil palm" (Source).


The following video from The Ecologist compliments the points made throughout this multimedia essay - reporting the current landscape of how elephants are suffering in Indonesia due to palm oil production:



"The Ecologist Film Unit travels to Indonesia to investigate the impact palm cultivation - and the resulting deforestation - is having on the Sumatran elephant, the world's most critically endangered elephant." (Source).


As a result of the issues (such as deforestation, pest control, and loss of resources) associated with palm oil, the population of Asian elephants is rapidly declining. There are less than 3000 Sumatran elephants left in the world (source). Additionally, there are only approximately 1500 Bornean Pygmy Elephants left in the wild (source).

Conclusion: The Severity of The Issue


  • "87% of primate species in Madagascar are threatened with extinction" (Source).

  • "Almost 80% of orangutan habitat has disappeared in the last 20 years" (Source).

  • "We are losing 6000 orangutans a year (Source).

  • "Indonesia's critically endangered orangutans are being killed at a rate of 25 a day" (Source).

  • "There are now only 400 Sumatran tigers left in the world" (Source).

  • "The last of Indonesia’s tigers — now fewer than 400 — are holding on for survival in the remaining patches of forests on the island of Sumatra" (Source).

  • "The elephant population across Sumatra is crashing, with far fewer than 3,000 surviving, as herds are left homeless, harassed and killed due to intense conflict with people over shrinking habitat" (Source).

  • "Only 1,500 or so of Bornean Pygmy Elephants still live on Borneo, an island where palm oil production continues to encroach on their habitat" (Source).
